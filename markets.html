What Energy Benchmarks Are and Why They Matter

A benchmark is a reference price used to value physical commodities and derivative contracts. WTI and Brent set reference prices for approximately 90% of globally traded crude oil. Henry Hub prices U.S. natural gas. RBOB sets U.S. wholesale gasoline. ULSD prices U.S. diesel and heating fuel. Each benchmark reflects deep liquidity, transparent price discovery, and market consensus on supply-demand balance.

Benchmarks move based on fundamental factors (supply disruptions, demand shifts, inventory changes, refinery operations, weather) and financial factors (speculative positioning, currency moves, risk appetite). A $1 move in WTI affects approximately $0.025/gallon at the U.S. pump. A $0.50 move in Henry Hub affects approximately $0.01/kWh in gas-fired electricity costs.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does the Markets page track?
The Markets dashboard tracks live prices for the most important global energy benchmarks: crude oil (WTI, Brent), natural gas (Henry Hub), refined products (gasoline RBOB, diesel/heating oil ULSD), plus reference prices for 150+ regional and international blends. Everything updates via API every 5 minutes for LIVE-labeled benchmarks.
Why do energy benchmarks move?
Benchmarks respond to supply disruptions, demand shifts, inventory data, weather, OPEC+ decisions, geopolitical events, currency moves, and trader positioning. In the current market, the Strait of Hormuz crisis is the dominant factor, adding a $20-25 risk premium to global crude and pressuring natural gas through LNG trade dynamics.
What is the difference between spot and futures pricing?
Spot prices reflect immediate physical delivery. Futures prices reflect agreed delivery at a specific future date. The hero cards on this page show front-month futures (the most actively traded contract). The difference between spot and futures indicates market expectations — backwardation signals tight supply, contango signals surplus.
Why are some benchmarks more important than others?
Benchmarks matter based on the volume of trade priced off them, liquidity in the futures market, and geographic relevance. Brent prices roughly 75% of globally traded oil. WTI dominates U.S. domestic crude. Henry Hub sets North American gas. RBOB and ULSD set U.S. refined product prices. Regional benchmarks (Dubai, WCS, Urals) serve specific trade flows.
How do these prices affect gas, diesel, and heating costs?
WTI/Brent moves flow to wholesale gasoline (RBOB) and diesel (ULSD) within days and to pump prices in 1-2 weeks. Henry Hub gas prices flow to electricity bills and heating costs within weeks. A $10/barrel crude increase adds $0.24-0.30/gallon at the pump.
How often does this page update?
The 5 hero benchmarks and the movers table update every 5 minutes via OilPriceAPI using real CME/ICE exchange data. Regional crude grades, OPEC blends, and international benchmarks update from their respective reporting channels with varying delays.
