Oil prices plunge as Iran says Strait of Hormuz 'open' during ceasefire

Staff Apr 19, 2026 5 min read

Crude benchmarks declined sharply following Iranian assurances that maritime traffic through the critical chokepoint would remain unobstructed during an active ceasefire. The statement eased concerns about potential supply disruptions in one of the world’s most strategically important waterways, through which roughly one-third of global seaborne oil passes daily. Market sentiment shifted from a risk premium built into prices toward expectations of stable regional flows.

The Strait of Hormuz has long been a focal point for energy traders and geopolitical analysts, given its vulnerability to closure or blockade during periods of heightened tension. Any prolonged disruption would immediately constrain global oil supply and trigger sharp price spikes; conversely, credible signals of continued access reduce such geopolitical premiums. Iran’s public commitment to keeping the strait open during the ceasefire period directly addresses that tail-risk concern.

Refineries and traders across Asia, Europe, and beyond closely monitor the waterway’s status, as disruptions would force costly rerouting of shipments around the Cape of Good Hope. The repricing lower reflects market confidence that oil flows will continue uninterrupted, at least for the duration of the current ceasefire arrangement. Any reversal in that commitment could swiftly reverse today’s declines.

Broader market dynamics—including global supply balances, demand seasonality, and currency movements—will continue to shape price direction beyond geopolitical developments. However, the removal of acute Strait of Hormuz closure risk represents a meaningful shift in the outlook for near-term crude availability and cost. Market participants will remain vigilant for any changes to Iranian or regional statements that could signal a return to heightened tensions.
