Oil prices plunge as Iran says Strait of Hormuz 'open' during ceasefire

Staff Apr 20, 2026 5 min read

Crude oil fell sharply on Friday after Iranian officials stated the Strait of Hormuz remained open and navigable amid a regional ceasefire agreement. The announcement eased concerns about potential supply disruptions through one of the world’s most critical chokepoints, through which roughly one-fifth of global seaborne oil passes daily. Market participants had priced in a risk premium ahead of the statement, and that buffer compressed as geopolitical tensions appeared to recede.

The Strait of Hormuz has long been a flashpoint for oil market volatility, given its strategic importance and history of tension between Iran and Western powers. Any threat to transit through the narrow waterway typically triggers sharp rallies in both WTI and Brent crude, as traders worry about supply cutoffs to global markets. Friday’s assurance from Tehran that shipping lanes would remain clear removed a significant downside risk to crude supply.

Energy markets are now focused on the durability of the ceasefire and whether regional stability will hold in coming weeks. If tensions ease further, traders may unwind additional geopolitical risk premiums embedded in crude prices. Conversely, any escalation or breakdown in the agreement could quickly reverse today’s losses.

Refiners and shippers will likely monitor official statements from Iran and other regional actors closely in the coming days. A sustained period of calm would allow markets to refocus on traditional supply–demand fundamentals, OPEC+ production decisions, and global growth forecasts. For now, the relief from immediate geopolitical concern has clearly dominated trading sentiment.
