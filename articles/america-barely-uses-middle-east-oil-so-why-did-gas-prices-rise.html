America barely uses Middle East oil. So why did gas prices rise?

Staff Apr 27, 2026 5 min read

U.S. refineries import less than 10% of crude from the Middle East, yet American motorists often feel the ripple effects of supply disruptions in that region. The reason is straightforward: global oil markets are interconnected. When geopolitical tensions reduce Middle Eastern crude flowing to Europe or Asia, buyers in those regions compete for alternative supplies, including barrels that might otherwise go to the U.S. or lower in price.

Brent crude—the international benchmark priced off North Sea production—sets the tone for global refining margins and ultimately pump prices across the Atlantic. A shock to Middle Eastern exports, whether from conflict, OPEC+ production cuts, or shipping disruptions through the Strait of Hormuz, tightens worldwide supply and pushes Brent higher. WTI, the U.S. benchmark, typically trades at a discount but still tracks Brent’s direction over time.

The U.S. energy system is also deeply integrated with global refining networks. American refineries export gasoline and diesel to Latin America and Europe while importing crude from diverse sources. A supply shock abroad can divert product flows, tighten regional inventories, and increase the cost of refined fuels domestically—even if no Middle Eastern oil physically reaches American shores.

Energy markets operate on expectations as much as current flows. Reports of production cuts or unrest in the Gulf can trigger immediate price moves as traders adjust positions for future scarcity. These moves happen in real time, before physical barrels even change hands. For U.S. consumers, the headline risk alone can translate into higher prices at the pump, regardless of how much crude actually originates in the Middle East.
