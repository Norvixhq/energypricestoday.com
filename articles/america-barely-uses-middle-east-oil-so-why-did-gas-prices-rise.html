America barely uses Middle East oil. So why did gas prices rise?

Staff Apr 29, 2026 5 min read

U.S. crude oil imports from the Middle East have fallen to historic lows, yet American drivers still see pump prices move in lockstep with supply disruptions in the Persian Gulf. This apparent disconnect reflects a fundamental misunderstanding about how global oil markets work. The price of gasoline in the U.S. isn’t set by what arrives at American refineries—it’s set by the global price of crude oil, regardless of where that oil comes from.

When tension or conflict disrupts Middle Eastern production, global crude inventories tighten and world oil prices rise immediately. Refineries worldwide compete for the same barrels, pushing benchmark prices like Brent crude higher. American refineries then buy crude at these elevated global prices, and those costs flow directly to the pump. The origin of the oil matters far less than the total supply available to the planet.

OPEC+ decisions also affect U.S. gasoline prices through the same mechanism. When Saudi Arabia or other Gulf producers adjust production quotas—or when geopolitical events constrain their output—global prices shift. American motorists pay the global market rate, not a domestic rate insulated from Middle Eastern dynamics. A 1 million barrel-per-day cut by OPEC+ in the Persian Gulf has the same upward pressure on pump prices as a comparable disruption in the North Sea or West Africa.

The U.S. energy market is deeply integrated into global crude flows and pricing, despite lower direct imports from the Middle East. Switching to renewable energy or domestic production offers long-term insulation from geopolitical shocks, but as long as the American economy runs on oil traded on global markets, Persian Gulf supply disruptions will continue to affect Main Street prices. Understanding this relationship helps explain why international energy security—not just American oil supply—remains central to U.S. energy policy.
