Here's what's behind oil's 8-day climb back to Iran-war highs

Staff Apr 29, 2026 5 min read

Crude markets have staged a sustained rally over the past week, driven by a confluence of geopolitical tension, supply tightness, and shifting demand signals across Asia and Europe. The move reflects renewed concerns about Middle East stability and the potential for production disruptions at a time when global inventories remain lean. Trading sentiment has shifted markedly from the demand-destruction fears that dominated sentiment earlier in the year.

Geopolitical risk premiums have re-emerged as a dominant market force, with traders pricing in the possibility of supply interruptions through critical chokepoints like the Strait of Hormuz. Regional escalations and sanctions-related supply constraints have reinforced the perception that the global oil market lacks adequate spare capacity to absorb any significant outage. These factors have lifted both WTI and Brent crude meaningfully from their lows, creating the conditions for the strongest sustained move in weeks.

On the demand side, signals from major consuming regions have improved relative to recent months, with refiners gradually increasing runs and storage draws accelerating in key markets. Chinese purchasing activity has picked up modestly, though uncertainty around broader economic growth continues to cap the magnitude of any bullish move. The combination of tighter balances and reduced hedging flows has created a self-reinforcing dynamic that pushes prices higher.

The rally remains vulnerable to shifting headlines and any signs of economic slowdown in developed markets, which could rapidly reverse the sentiment gains of the past eight days. Energy traders are closely monitoring both geopolitical developments and upcoming data on global inventory levels, as either could become a pivot point for the current move. For now, the market appears to be pricing a baseline of elevated risk premium and structural tightness rather than immediate supply disruption.
