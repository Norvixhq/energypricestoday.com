America barely uses OPEC oil. Why are gas prices so high?

Staff Apr 29, 2026 5 min read

U.S. crude imports from OPEC members represent roughly 6–8% of total American oil supply, yet global oil prices remain the primary driver of pump prices at home. Domestic crude production now exceeds 10 million barrels per day, and the U.S. imports from a diverse set of suppliers including Canada, Brazil, and West Africa. The disconnect between OPEC’s modest share of U.S. supplies and America’s vulnerability to global price swings reflects how interconnected energy markets have become.

Crude oil trades on global benchmarks—primarily WTI and Brent—regardless of where it originates or where it will be refined. When OPEC+ adjusts production or geopolitical tensions tighten supply elsewhere, those benchmark prices move, and U.S. refiners pay global rates for feedstock. Even if an American refinery sources crude from non-OPEC suppliers, it must compete for that barrel at world prices, ensuring that supply shocks ripple across all markets instantly.

Refining capacity and product exports add another layer of complexity. The U.S. runs significant spare refining capacity and exports gasoline and diesel globally, meaning domestic consumption competes with international demand. Maintenance outages at key U.S. refineries, seasonal shifts in product demand, and logistics bottlenecks can tighten supplies independent of crude sourcing. These bottlenecks often matter more to pump prices than the crude barrel’s origin.

Geopolitical risk premiums embedded in global oil prices also affect American consumers directly. Tensions in the Strait of Hormuz, supply disruptions in the Atlantic, or production decisions by major exporters all influence the WTI benchmark that U.S. retailers use to price gasoline. Because global energy markets are unified by price, not by bilateral trade flows, America’s energy independence on crude supply does not insulate it from world events.
