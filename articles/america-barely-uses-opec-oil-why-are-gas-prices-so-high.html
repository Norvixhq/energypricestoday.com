America barely uses OPEC oil. Why are gas prices so high?

Staff Apr 30, 2026 5 min read

U.S. crude oil imports from OPEC members have declined steadily over the past decade, falling to historic lows as domestic shale production surged and refinery capacity expanded. Today, OPEC supplies roughly 6–8% of America’s crude intake, a far smaller share than in the 2000s. Yet gasoline prices remain sensitive to global crude benchmarks like WTI and Brent, which respond to OPEC production decisions and broader supply–demand dynamics.

The disconnect exists because oil is a globally traded commodity priced at the margin. Even if the U.S. produces more crude than it imports, American refineries process crude at world prices, and those prices reflect OPEC’s output levels, geopolitical tensions, and global demand. When OPEC+ cuts production or supply tightens elsewhere—such as disruptions in the North Sea or Middle East—benchmark prices rise, raising fuel costs at U.S. pumps regardless of where America sources its oil.

Refining capacity and infrastructure also matter. U.S. refineries are optimized for medium and heavy crude, much of which comes from Canada, Mexico, and the Middle East. If OPEC restricts supply, refineries face higher input costs or must switch to costlier alternatives, both of which push gasoline prices upward. Additionally, global LNG markets and fuel demand from Asia and Europe create competing bidders for available barrels.

The bottom line: energy markets are interconnected. American motorists pay world prices because oil trades globally, and OPEC’s influence on those prices persists even as U.S. import dependence has shrunk. Domestic production insulates the country from some external shocks, but it does not decouple America from the global crude market.
