Oil prices plunge 11% after Iran says Strait of Hormuz is open for commercial vessels

Staff Apr 20, 2026 5 min read

Crude benchmarks fell sharply following Iran’s announcement that commercial shipping corridors through the Strait of Hormuz remain operational. The statement eased concerns about potential disruptions to one of the world’s most critical energy choke points, through which roughly one-third of globally traded seaborne oil passes daily. Market participants had priced in a risk premium ahead of the announcement, reflecting heightened geopolitical tension in the region.

The decline signals trader relief after days of elevated volatility tied to Middle East developments. When supply route security comes into question, energy markets typically respond with sharp upward moves as investors demand compensation for disruption risk. The reversal in this case suggests the immediate threat to transit has receded, at least according to official Iranian channels.

Strait of Hormuz security remains a cornerstone of oil market fundamentals, given its role in connecting Persian Gulf producers to global demand. Any sustained closure would rapidly reshape crude logistics and create severe supply pressures on refiners worldwide. The waterway’s status typically influences both WTI and Brent pricing, making Iranian statements on its accessibility material to market participants.

While the announcement prompted today’s selloff, traders will likely continue monitoring the situation closely for any new developments. Geopolitical risk premiums in crude markets can return quickly if conditions change or official assurances are contradicted by events. The energy sector remains attentive to further signals from Tehran and broader regional stability indicators.
