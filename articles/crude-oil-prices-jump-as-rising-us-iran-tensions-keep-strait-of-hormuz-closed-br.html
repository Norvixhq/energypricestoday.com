Crude oil prices jump as rising US-Iran tensions keep Strait of Hormuz closed; Brent oil above $95 a barrel

Staff Apr 20, 2026 5 min read

Geopolitical tensions between the United States and Iran have intensified shipping restrictions through the Strait of Hormuz, a critical chokepoint that handles roughly one-fifth of global crude exports. The closure is directly supporting oil valuations, with Brent crude climbing above $95 per barrel as traders price in supply disruption risk. This marks a meaningful move in a volatile trading environment already shaped by OPEC+ output decisions and global demand signals.

The Strait of Hormuz remains one of the world’s most strategically sensitive energy corridors; even temporary transit delays can amplify price pressures across crude markets within hours. Insurance premiums for tanker passage have risen, and some shippers are rerouting vessels through longer, costlier alternatives. This friction in the physical market has supported both Brent and WTI crude, though the degree of actual supply loss remains unclear.

Elevated crude prices benefit energy producers with spare capacity but raise concerns for refining margins and downstream fuel costs. Consumers and fuel importers are watching closely to see whether tensions ease or escalate further. The broader market is also assessing how long shipping disruptions might persist and whether they trigger strategic reserve releases from major consuming nations.

Energy traders are monitoring official statements from regional powers and the U.S. for signals about de-escalation or further restrictions. A sustained closure would likely keep crude supported near current levels, while any breakthrough in negotiations could prompt sharp profit-taking. For now, the Strait situation remains the dominant price driver for crude markets.
