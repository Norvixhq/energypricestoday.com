U.S. oil hovers near $100 on report Trump dissatisfied with Iran's proposal to open Hormuz

Staff Apr 29, 2026 5 min read

West Texas Intermediate crude traded near the $100 per barrel mark on Tuesday as investors weighed fresh tensions between Washington and Tehran over regional shipping corridors. The geopolitical premium has remained intact amid broader uncertainty about Middle East stability and crude export routes. Market participants are closely monitoring how diplomatic friction could affect the roughly 21 million barrels per day that transit the Strait of Hormuz annually.

According to reports, the Trump administration has rejected an Iranian proposal aimed at reopening shipping lanes through the strategic waterway. The nature of Iran’s proposal and specific objections remain unclear, but energy markets have historically moved sharply on any disruption threat to Hormuz traffic. Oil traders are pricing in elevated risk while awaiting further statements from U.S. officials or Iranian government representatives.

The $100 level has become a psychological benchmark for crude traders, balancing supply concerns against demand forecasts. Prices have remained relatively stable in the $95–$105 range in recent weeks, reflecting competing pressures from global production levels and macroeconomic uncertainty. Any escalation in U.S.–Iran tensions could test resistance at higher levels.

Energy market participants will likely focus on official statements from both governments over the coming days, along with any signs of increased naval activity near the Strait. Refineries and traders dependent on Middle Eastern crude flows are bracing for volatility. The outcome of these diplomatic developments could significantly influence oil prices and broader energy market sentiment heading into year-end.
