U.S. Gas Prices Climb Further as Effects of Iran War Reverberate

Staff Apr 30, 2026 5 min read

Gasoline prices at U.S. pumps have edged higher over the past week as regional supply chains and global crude benchmarks respond to escalating tensions in the Middle East. The uptick reflects growing concern that disruptions to Iranian oil exports—or retaliatory action affecting the Strait of Hormuz—could tighten global markets. Refineries are monitoring inventory levels closely, and traders have begun pricing in a geopolitical risk premium to both WTI and Brent crude.

Iran produces roughly 3.2 million barrels per day of crude oil, a significant volume in global supply balances. Any material loss of Iranian barrels would likely require OPEC+ producers to consider output adjustments, though spare capacity among Gulf members remains limited. Traders are also watching shipping routes through the Strait of Hormuz, where roughly one-third of global seaborne oil passes daily.

The impact on U.S. consumers depends partly on refinery utilization rates and regional grade preferences. The Gulf Coast refining complex, which typically processes light sweet crude, may face margin pressures if Brent outpaces WTI more sharply. Retail gas prices tend to lag wholesale moves by several days to weeks, so current station prices may not fully reflect recent market shifts.

Energy analysts expect volatility to persist until the geopolitical situation clarifies. The timing of any potential supply losses—and whether they are partial or prolonged—remains uncertain. For now, traders are maintaining elevated risk premiums, which supports both crude futures and downstream products including gasoline and diesel.
