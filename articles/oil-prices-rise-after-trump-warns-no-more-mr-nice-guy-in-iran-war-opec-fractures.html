Oil Prices Rise After Trump Warns ‘No More Mr Nice Guy’ in Iran War, OPEC Fractures

Staff Apr 29, 2026 5 min read

Crude oil benchmarks moved higher Tuesday following a sharp escalation in U.S. rhetoric toward Iran, which traders interpreted as a signal of increased geopolitical risk in the Middle East. The comments, coupled with longstanding tensions over regional shipping lanes and nuclear negotiations, pushed risk premiums back into energy markets after weeks of relative calm. Traders noted that any disruption to Iranian oil exports or broader regional instability would tighten global supply, particularly given existing production constraints elsewhere.

The price reaction underscored a broader challenge facing OPEC+: coordinating production policy amid deepening internal fractures. Saudi Arabia and the UAE have clashed repeatedly over output quotas and compliance measures, while Russia’s continued participation remains uncertain given Western sanctions and its focus on Eastern markets. These divisions have weakened the group’s ability to manage supply and defend prices through a unified strategy.

From a market mechanics standpoint, geopolitical premiums tend to be volatile and difficult to sustain without tangible supply disruptions. Energy traders are closely monitoring the Strait of Hormuz, through which roughly one-fifth of global crude oil passes, as any incident there could rapidly accelerate price moves. For now, the market appears to be pricing in elevated tail risk rather than a near-term, material supply shock.

Upstream and refining markets will likely remain sensitive to both diplomatic developments and OPEC+ production decisions in the coming weeks. Investors should watch for any signals regarding Iranian sanctions enforcement, Saudi-UAE cooperation on output, and broader U.S. energy policy under the new administration. The convergence of geopolitical risk and cartel fragmentation leaves price direction uncertain, though fundamentals remain the ultimate driver over longer horizons.
